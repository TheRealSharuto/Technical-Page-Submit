What is UX design?

UX means "the user experience". "User Experience" refers to how a person, the user, feels about interacting with or experiencing a product. User experience (UX) design is the process design teams use to create products that provide meaningful and relevant experiences to users. This involves the design of the entire process of acquiring and integrating the product, including aspects of branding, design, usability and function.

UX designers design many things - from physical to digital products. It might be a physical product like a video game controller or a bag of potato chips. They may also design tech products such as apps, websites, or smartwatches.

The UX designer's job is to have all users have a good experience. Therefore, their products need to be useable, equitable, enjoyable, and useful. In other words, the UX designer must improve products by making them easier to use. They make the design, structure, and purpose of the design clear to everyone. Then, they consider the unique needs of many different people in order to make equitable designs, thus, the designs are made useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities and backgrounds.

All in all, UX designers have the purpose of creating a positive connection between the user and the product by taking a user's thoughts and feelings into consideration. All of this is achieved with a team of specialists of different backgrounds - Graphic Designers, UX Designeres, UX Researchers, UX Writers, Production Designers, Production Designers, Assets, UX engineers, UX Program managers, Financial advisors, and many more! Many individuals have to work in a team and do their part in order for ideas for the best user-friendly designs to be put on the market.

What is a Design Sprint?

A Design Sprint is a time-based process wth five phases typically spread over 5 full 8-hour days.

The goal of a Design Sprint is to solve a problem through designing, prototyping, and testing ideas with users.

Don't know if you should do a Design Sprint? Ask these three questions.

      1. Are there many potential solutions to your design challenge?

      2. Does a design challenge require people from cross-functional teams to weigh in?

      3. Is the design challenge's scope wide enough for a sprint?
